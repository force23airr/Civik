# Civik — Business Model & Revenue Strategy

---

## How Waze Makes Money (For Context)

Waze doesn't pay drivers anything. Drivers contribute data for free (police sightings, traffic, hazards) and Waze monetizes it through:

1. **Location-based ads** — businesses pay to show ads when drivers pass nearby ("Dunkin' Donuts, 0.2 miles ahead")
2. **Waze for Cities** — governments pay for access to aggregated traffic data
3. **Google integration** — Waze data feeds into Google Maps (Google bought them for $1.1B in 2013)

Drivers get zero dollars. They contribute because it's useful to them personally.

## Why Would Towns Pay for Pothole Reports?

They already do — just not through an app like Civik yet. Here's the reality:

**Towns already spend huge money finding potholes:**
- They pay full-time employees to drive around and survey roads
- They respond to 311 phone calls (staffing those call centers costs money)
- They get sued when someone damages their car on an unreported pothole (lawsuit payouts are $5K-$50K+ each)
- They hire engineering firms to do road condition assessments ($50K-$200K per study)

**A $5 bounty to a driver is insanely cheap compared to:**
- A $50K lawsuit from a blown tire on an unreported pothole
- A $70K/year salary for a road inspector
- A $100K road assessment contract

The bounty isn't charity — it's the cheapest pothole detection system a city could ever buy.

## How Civik Makes Money (Multiple Revenue Streams)

| Revenue Stream | Who Pays | Why |
|---|---|---|
| **City data subscriptions** | Municipalities | Live pothole map replaces expensive road surveys |
| **Platform fee on contractor bids** | Contractors (future) | 8-15% cut when a contractor wins a repair job |
| **Insurance data licensing** | Auto insurance companies | Verified accident hotspot data for risk pricing |
| **Fleet safety product** | Logistics companies | Real-time road hazard alerts for delivery fleets |
| **Location ads** (Waze model) | Local businesses | "Tire shop 0.3 miles ahead" when driving near a pothole |
| **Bounty sponsorships** | Cities or businesses | A city funds the bounty pool instead of paying inspectors |

---

## The Key Insight

Cities don't pay drivers directly. Civik pays the bounty from its own revenue, and cities pay Civik for the data. The driver doesn't care where the $5 comes from — they just see the payout. The city doesn't care that drivers are getting paid — they just see a cheaper, faster, more comprehensive pothole detection system than anything they could build internally.

It's the same model as Google paying Apple $20B/year to be the default search engine. The user (Apple) gets paid, the advertiser (Google) gets distribution, and the platform (Safari) connects them. In Civik's case: the driver gets paid, the city gets data, and Civik is the platform.
